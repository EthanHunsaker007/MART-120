WHITE

By: Ethan Hunsaker

    The spattered droplets of blood seemed especially red against the whiteness of the snow. They trailed behind the limping man—hot drips that burrowed into the powdery expanse. He paused for a moment on his path, leaning against the cold, rough bark of a young pine. He drew a ragged breath, the frigid air tearing at his lungs. Slowly, he unwrapped the blood-stained cloth covering his mangled arm.
    His eyes tightened. A half-strangled sigh escaped his lips.
    Back on went the tattered cloth.
    Out of his breast pocket, he drew a small compass. Still leaning against the tree, he slid down to a squat and placed the compass gingerly on the snow. Its needle wavered and shook until it was finally—hesitantly—pointing northward. His bearing had not changed much from his last stop; this pleased him. The compass was quickly delivered back to its pocket, and the soldier was again on his way. By his estimation, the small base from which he and his comrades had departed could not be more than ten klicks away. Though the sun was beginning to graze the peaks of the mountains visible just above the treetops, he was quite sure he would make it.
    A wolf’s howl echoed through the pines. The soldier instinctively reached for the comfortable grip of the pistol on his left hip. He shot up from his low squat and, after a quick moment to steady his palms, set off again into the taiga beyond. As he trekked through the darkening forest, a keening wind began to swirl a dusting of snow around his feet.
    The incessant drip of blood marked his slow passage.
    As his feet dragged endlessly on, his mind began to wander away from the biting cold and the towering trees. Instead it landed, as it often did, on the memory of home. He could almost feel the warmth from the crackling hearth seeping into his feet from the plush carpet below. His wife stood leaned against the doorway.
    “You don’t have to go.” She couldn’t look him in the eyes as she said it.
    “Who else will fight them, Sofia?”
    “There’s plenty of soldiers champing at the bit to go; they don’t need you!”
    “I need to go.” He couldn’t look her in the eyes as he said it.
    She turned away, and stared blankly out a rain-streaked window overlooking the city below. “Lucille will be grown when you come back. If you come back.” Her hand found its way absentmindedly to her rounded stomach.
    “By the time the war’s over Lucille will be barely done with the nursemaid, my love. And I promise—I swear, I’ll come back to you.”
    He had volunteered for service the next day.
    Another howl—closer than the first—broke him from his reverie. The shadows cast by the trees around him had lengthened considerably, and only the barest hint of the sun still shone over the mountaintops. He slowed to a stop once more, sagging against a nearby trunk. The air was still, save for the soldier’s labored breaths and the never-ending drip, drop of his blood on the snow below. Again, he reached for his compass. His path had not been quite so straight as before, and the bearing the instrument showed brought a tight grimace to his face. The wolf cried out again, even nearer still. The soldier drew his pistol warily, his eyes searching the darkness of the trees beyond. By now, the sun was set completely, and only a faint glow over the snowy peaks in the distance remained. He shook his head to clear it of the shadows and the cold beyond, and set out again into the forest.
    He clutched his pistol tightly as his head swiveled between the dark places amongst the trunks of the ancient pines. He was sure he saw a shape slipping in and out of view, but he couldn’t be certain if it was a trick of the cold. He knew well what the cold of these woods could do to a man.
    After an agonizing while, a howl shattered the silence, far too close. The soldier froze in his tracks, plumes of frozen air escaping from his lungs as he tried in vain to still his rapid breaths. He heard from the forest beyond the crunching of snow under feet that were not his. He waited.
    From behind a towering pine just ten meters in front of the man, a large, snuffling, blindingly white wolf slowly plodded forth. It walked with a strange gait; one leg dragged uselessly behind its lithe figure. It paused in its path, lifting its head in a slight cock. Its ears twitched and swiveled towards the still-frozen soldier.
    The crack of the pistol sent the wolf scampering back away into the darkness.
    The soldier stood in the small clearing where he had stopped, holding his pistol in both shaking hands. The sharp sound of the shot still rang in his ears. A small gash had opened in the pine to the left of the wolf, just a few inches above where its head had been held poised.
    Slowly, he lowered the gun, though he did not yet return it to its holster. He had, in truth, not even felt himself pull the trigger. It was not the first time that day he had fired that pistol.
    Something about the fading memory of the wolf’s face brought him back to another memory—a world of smoke and flashes and death he had occupied just earlier that day. He and his fellow soldiers had stumbled upon the tents on a routine patrol. There weren't too many men, but a scout from the camp had seen the patrol far before its path intersected theirs, and so when it did, the few men of that small camp greeted their patrol with open fire. The wounded soldier—who had not been that way before the firefight—had been in the rear of the march, and thus the last to realize what exactly they had happened upon. By the time he understood, there was nothing left to do but run. The shrapnel now lodged in his arm had come from a grenade hurled into the center of the group. He got away lucky. No one else could say the same.
    He sprinted as fast as his legs could take him, weaving through the thick trunks. Bullets burrowed into the pines behind him. It did not take long for him to be forgotten, as what few of his comrades remained fought futilely to their last breath. He only paused when he could no longer hear the shots ring out behind him.
    He found himself in a small, round opening in the dense trees. It was bright; the light bounced brilliantly off the whiteness of the snow. As he caught his breath in that clearing, he felt the sunbeams streaming through the pine boughs warm the skin on his face.
    A boot crunched in the snow behind him.
    He swiveled mechanically, the pistol leaving its holster at his side in a fluid motion. The boy was young—clumsy. He had fumbled with his oversized rifle as he brought it to his cheek, nearly dropping it, which caused him to take a small, involuntary step forward to regain his balance.
    The crack of the pistol sent the boy slumping to his knees, his rifle thudding softly from his hands onto the crimson snow below. The soldier watched as the stain in the snow around the prone form grew larger.
    He stood still, the pistol clutched in both still hands.
    He re-holstered the gun and drew out his compass again, returning slowly to the present. His head was bent low to read it; he squinted his eyes against the darkness. The needle’s direction was not promising. He had slowly been drifting east, and what started as a ten kilometer journey had stayed just that. He did not know this, of course, and had no other option but to make a vague correction and press on into the night.
    He could no longer feel his fingers or feet. The breeze that had previously swirled gently around him now whipped and tore, slicing into his back and chilling him to his core. Still, he walked. He was certain as he continued that he could feel the eyes of the white wolf boring into his back. His hand found its way to the grip of the pistol once more. His strides had shortened greatly; the soldier was reduced to short, shuffling steps through the endless snow. The bloodstained rag wrapped around his arm had frozen stiff in the wind, and he could no longer peel it back to peer at his wound. His breaths came short and quickly in the icy air, and he began to wonder for the first time if perhaps he would not make it home. He worried about breaking his promise to Sofia.
    His eyes swept upward from the snow to the starry sky above. This far into the forest, the constellations swirled and streaked across the black canvas of the night. His gaze returned to the forest around him, and with a slow start, he noticed the wolf plodding alongside him. It matched his limping gait, walking about five meters to his right. It looked over at him as they trudged through the snow with the same cocked, curious expression as before.
    The soldier tightened his grip around the pistol, but found he could not feel it in his grasp. His hand had slipped from its grip long ago, and hung weakly, still at his side. His shivering had ceased, he noticed. He looked up at the wolf again. He stopped walking. The wolf stopped, too.
    He gazed across the distance between them into the creature’s black eyes.
    Its lame paw left a furrow in the snow behind as it dragged along. The soldier’s gaze searched the wolf’s face. His expression hardened. He would not be made a mockery by this wounded animal. His comrades had not died for him to give up, alone in the snow.
    He mustered some strength into the fingers of his right arm and delved them into the pocket of his coat. Clumsily, he drew out the small, bloodstained scrap of paper secreted away within. This was what his fellows had died for. He unfolded it gently, gazing at the names arranged in neatly inked rows. As carefully as he could, he placed the piece of paper into his trouser pocket and turned away from the wolf.
    He could carry on easily, he thought, if only he wasn’t so hot. His leaden fingers clawed weakly at the collar of his jacket, his body suddenly burning with a newfound warmth. As quick as his clumsy fingers could, he unbuttoned his grey coat and folded it neatly on the snow to his right. It did not stand out, save for the bright red band circling its left sleeve.
    He only made it fifteen meters more into the forest before he collapsed into the soft snow below. The intelligence he carried in his pocket—a small scrap of bloodstained paper, marked with the insignia of the SS—would never make it back to his superiors.
    The wolf sat staring at the prone figure for a long while. Eventually, though, it turned back the way it had come, and set off slowly into the forest. The soldier’s body was left untouched where it had fallen.
    Overhead, the stars shone brightly from their place on high. The currents of the galaxy were visible just faintly in the perfect blackness, weaving and winding through the cosmos. The stars continued their slow dance until the sun began to rise on the snowy forests below.
    As the first sunbeams crest the mountains and stream through the branches of the towering pines, they find a lone soldier, dead in the snow.


Note: I wrote this story for another class, but it is entirely original.
